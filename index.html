Chapter 01

The brain is
rewired to
want more.

"Addiction isn't a moral failure. It's a hijacking ... of the brain's most primal survival system."

Every drug of abuse floods the brain's reward system with dopamine ... sometimes 2 to 10 times more than any natural pleasure. The brain, designed to repeat survival behaviours, begins to treat drug use as necessary for life itself.

Over time, natural pleasure fades. Food, connection, joy ... they register as nothing. Only the substance remains vivid. This is not weakness. This is neurological capture.

Understanding this is the first step to fighting it.

Chapter 03 ... The Descent

First use ... Day 1
The Rush
Dopamine floods the reward pathway. The brain records this as an event more pleasurable than anything natural. A blueprint for craving is laid down immediately.
Risk Level

The Return
The brain begins downregulating dopamine receptors. Normal life feels grey. The drug is now chased not for highs, but to feel normal. Frequency increases.
Risk Level
Months 2–6
The Tolerance
Higher doses are required for the same effect. The prefrontal cortex ... seat of judgment and self-control ... begins losing grey matter. Decisions change.
Risk Level

The Dependency
Physical withdrawal begins without the substance. Relationships fracture. Employment suffers. The drug is no longer a choice ... it is a biological demand. Quitting alone becomes medically dangerous.

Chapter 05 ... The Body's Damage

What drugs
destroy inside.

Affected organs ... scroll to explore
Brain
The command centre goes dark.
Long-term drug use causes measurable brain shrinkage. The prefrontal cortex ... responsible for impulse control, decision-making, and empathy ... loses grey matter. The hippocampus, which forms memories, shrinks. Dopamine receptors disappear as the brain tries to cope with overstimulation. The result: a brain that can no longer feel pleasure normally, cannot make good decisions, and cannot form new memories as effectively.

Methamphetamine destroys up to 11% of neurons in the limbic system. Cocaine can trigger strokes in people as young as 19. Inhalants destroy the myelin sheath protecting neurons ... equivalent to burning your wiring.
Meth Cocaine Heroin Alcohol Inhalants
Heart & Cardiovascular System
The engine pushed past its limits.
Stimulants force the heart to beat faster and harder than it's designed to. Cocaine constricts blood vessels while simultaneously accelerating the heart ... the leading cause of cocaine-related deaths. Even a single use can trigger myocardial infarction in healthy individuals.

Chronic meth use causes cardiomyopathy ... the heart muscle weakens and enlarges, losing its ability to pump effectively. MDMA causes a dangerous rise in blood pressure and temperature, sometimes leading to cardiac arrest. Opioids suppress the signal to breathe.
Cocaine Meth MDMA Heroin
Liver
The filter that cannot filter itself.
The liver is the body's primary detoxification organ, and drugs are exactly the toxins it's supposed to process. But at chronic levels, the liver is overwhelmed. Hepatitis C spreads through shared needles and infects 3 out of 4 long-term intravenous drug users. Cirrhosis ... irreversible scarring ... follows alcohol and drug abuse reliably.

MDMA in high doses can cause acute liver failure in a single session. Acetaminophen, often mixed with opioids, destroys liver cells. Steroids cause peliosis hepatis ... blood-filled cysts that can rupture catastrophically.
Alcohol MDMA Steroids Opioids
Lungs & Respiratory System
Every breath becomes harder.
Smoked drugs ... crack cocaine, meth, heroin ... destroy the tiny air sacs (alveoli) that transfer oxygen into the blood. Crack lung causes massive pulmonary hemorrhage. Smoking meth causes severe respiratory failure. Cannabis smoke, while less studied, contains many of the same carcinogens as tobacco.

Opioids and benzodiazepines suppress the brainstem signal that tells you to breathe. In overdose, users simply stop breathing ... quietly, without pain, in their sleep.
Crack Cocaine Heroin Meth Benzodiazepines
Skin & Appearance
The face of addiction is unmistakable.
Meth users experience formication ... the hallucination of insects crawling under the skin ... leading to compulsive scratching that creates open wounds. The "meth mouth" phenomenon results from dry mouth combined with teeth grinding and poor hygiene; teeth blacken and crumble within months.

Heroin users show collapsed, scarred veins. Track marks from intravenous injection become infected, leading to abscesses. Cocaine damages the nasal septum ... 10% of chronic users lose it entirely. Crack cocaine ages skin dramatically through vasoconstriction.
